# The Warmth of Clay: Why Handmade Matters

My hands are almost always stained with clay. Under my fingernails, in the creases of my palms, a fine dust that never quite washes away. I've stopped trying to have perfectly clean hands.

They tell the story of the work.

## The Speed of Making

It takes **three weeks** to make a bowl.

Not three weeks of continuous work—the clay needs time to dry, the kiln needs time to heat and cool, the glaze needs time to set. But from the moment I wedge the clay to the moment a finished bowl emerges from the final firing, it's never less than three weeks.

In a world where you can click a button and have a product arrive the next day, this slowness feels **radical**.

## What Gets Lost in Speed

Mass production is miraculous in its own way. But something gets lost in the efficiency:

- The **slight variation** that makes each piece unique
- The **human touch**—literally, the fingerprints in the clay
- The **story** of who made it and why
- The **care** that comes from creating one piece at a time

When I make a bowl, I'm thinking about who might use it. What it will hold. How it will feel in someone's hands during their morning routine, their dinner ritual, their quiet moment with tea.

## The Imperfection Principle

Here's what I've learned: **the imperfections are what make it beautiful.**

A perfectly uniform bowl, made by machine, is impressive. But a bowl with slight variations—a wobble in the rim, a thicker glaze on one side, an unexpected color where glazes overlapped—that bowl has **character**.

It's not trying to be perfect. It's trying to be **itself**.

## Connection Across Distance

When you use a handmade piece, there's an invisible thread connecting you to its maker. You're holding something someone else held. Shaped with their hands. Fired in their kiln. Packed with care.

That connection matters more than we realize.

## The Choice

Every time you choose handmade over mass-produced, you're voting for:

- **Slower, more intentional creation**
- **Individual makers** supporting themselves through craft
- **Quality and durability** over disposability
- **Beauty that ages** rather than beauty that fades

You're saying: *I want the things I live with to have stories.*
